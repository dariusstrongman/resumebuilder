How the score works

The checker computes four signals and weights them into one 0-100 score.

  • Action verb ratio. Bullets that start with strong verbs (Built, Led, Shipped, Reduced, Scaled) outperform bullets that start with "Responsible for" or "Worked on". A high ratio means the resume reads as ownership and outcomes, not job description copy.
  • Passive voice. "The system was redesigned by me" is weaker than "I redesigned the system". Recruiters read passive voice as evasion. Lower is better.
  • Sentence length. The optimal range for resume bullets is roughly 12-18 words. Longer than 25 reads as run-on; shorter than 8 reads as filler.
  • Quantifier density. Numbers, percentages, dollar amounts, time savings. The single biggest separator between resumes that get interviews and ones that don't. The score rewards specific quantification.

What to do with the score

Scores in the 80s and 90s are publication-ready: every bullet starts with a verb, most have a number attached, sentences are tight. Scores in the 60-79 range usually need 5-10 specific rewrites: passive lines flipped to active, vague ones quantified. Below 60, the bullets need a structural pass: most are reading as job-description filler, not accomplishment.

Pair this with our free ATS grader for the parser-side view. Readability is the recruiter's first impression. ATS-friendliness is whether the recruiter ever sees it.

Limits of readability scoring

The score doesn't know your industry. A federal resume legitimately uses longer sentences. A creative-director resume uses voice. A technical resume uses terminology that hurts readability indices but is correct for the audience. Use the score as a directional check, not gospel.

The score also can't tell you whether your bullets are interesting. "Built dashboard for 200 users" is readable but boring. "Cut weekly reporting time from 3 hours to 9 minutes for 200 analysts" is readable AND compelling. That second bullet only happens by remembering specifics.
